EZLynx vs Applied Epic vs HawkSoft vs AMS360: A Practitioner's Comparison for Commercial-Lines Workflow Automation

The four AMS platforms most independent commercial agencies run on, evaluated on the one axis that determines daily operations: how far they get you toward commercial workflow automation, and where you have to build around them.

Drrizo Research April 22, 2026 · 18 min read

Every agency management system comparison article written by the AMS vendors themselves, by the trade publications, and by the major consultancies tends toward the same conclusion: "it depends on your agency's needs." This is true in the way that any statement applied broadly is true, and it is useless in the way that broad statements applied to specific decisions are useless. Independent commercial agency owners evaluating an AMS migration — or, more commonly, trying to decide what workflow layer to build on top of their existing AMS — need a sharper answer than "it depends." They need to know which platform actually handles commercial workflow natively, which one leaves gaps that have to be filled externally, and which gaps are tolerable versus which are structural.

This article takes a sharper position. It is written from the vantage of someone who builds workflow automation on top of these four systems for commercial-focused independent agencies — meaning we have watched what each platform does well, what each does poorly, and where the architectural constraints actually live. The four platforms covered here — EZLynx, Applied Epic, HawkSoft, and AMS360 — are the systems commercial agencies in the $500K to $1.5M commission range actually run. Newer entrants (Momentum/NowCerts, QQCatalyst, Vertafore's newer offerings) are mentioned at the end; they are not yet the majority of what the market actually operates.

The evaluation axes are specific. We look at native commercial workflow depth, API and automation extensibility, COI generation quality, commission reconciliation capability, integration ceiling with external tools, and the realistic total cost of operating a commercial book on the platform. The verdict on each is not a star rating. It is an answer to the question: for a commercial-focused independent agency, where does this platform leave you, and what does the workflow layer you build around it have to do?

The Frame: What Commercial Workflow Actually Requires

Before evaluating any AMS, it is worth naming what commercial workflow actually requires — because most AMS marketing is built around personal-lines scenarios (fast quoting, carrier downloads, customer portals) that only partially overlap with commercial operations. A commercial agency runs on a different set of core workflows:

Certificate of insurance generation at scale, with accurate auto-population from policy data, endorsement-number references, and support for construction-specific language requirements. Volume is high; accuracy requirements are higher.

Structured new business submission packaging, including ACORD 125/126/140 population from intake data, supplemental application handling, and submission to multiple carrier markets in parallel. The workflow is data-intensive and repeats many times per new account.

Renewal stewardship across long windows, typically 90 to 120 days, involving structured pre-renewal data collection, exposure questionnaires, and coordinated producer/account manager touchpoints.

Commission reconciliation against direct-bill carrier statements arriving in varying formats, reconciled to policy-level entries in the AMS, with exceptions surfaced for review.

Cross-sell trigger detection across the book — payroll growth, new locations, missing coverage lines — converted into producer tasks at the moment they become actionable.

Almost no independent commercial agency gets all of these from their AMS alone. The question is which workflows the AMS handles well, which it handles badly, and which it does not handle at all.

EZLynx: The Default for Small-to-Mid Commercial

EZLynx is the most common AMS in the small-to-mid independent agency market, and for personal-lines-heavy or split-book agencies it is a capable and cost-efficient choice. The platform's strength is in its real-time comparative rater, its integrated email marketing module, and its relatively approachable pricing. For agencies in the $500K to $1.5M commission range running a mixed book, it is often the pragmatic default.

The story changes when the book tilts commercial. EZLynx's commercial-lines support has historically been thinner than its personal-lines support, and several specific gaps show up repeatedly in commercial-focused agencies running on the platform.

COI generation on EZLynx is functional but produces certificates that require meaningful manual review before issuance to compliance-portal-using GCs. The endorsement field is typically filled via checkbox rather than by-number reference, and the description-of-operations box is not tightly linked to contract-specific requirements. Construction-focused agencies on EZLynx routinely run first-pass rejection rates north of 50% without a workflow layer.

Commercial new business submission support is workable for single-line placements but starts to strain when submissions require multiple ACORD forms, supplemental applications, and multi-market placement. The packaging workflow is manual enough that a CSR spends two to four hours per complex submission.

API and automation extensibility has improved in recent years with EZLynx Connect, but the platform remains relatively constrained compared to Applied Epic. Custom workflow automation beyond what EZLynx ships natively generally requires routing data out of the AMS into external tools.

Commission reconciliation is adequate for personal-lines direct-bill patterns. For commercial direct-bill statements arriving from multiple carriers in varied formats, the native reconciliation is insufficient; most agencies handle this outside the AMS in spreadsheets or purpose-built tools.

EZLYNX · VERDICT

Capable default for smaller commercial books; substantial workflow layer required above $750K commercial commission

EZLynx works well for commercial agencies running below roughly $750K in commercial commission on a mixed book. Above that threshold — particularly for construction-heavy or specialty-line-heavy agencies — the gaps in COI generation, submission packaging, and commission reconciliation become material, and the cost of the workflow layer required to close them is real.

Best for: Mixed personal/commercial agencies under $750K commercial commission. Agencies on a budget. Agencies where the principal is comfortable operating outside the AMS for complex commercial tasks.

Applied Epic: The Commercial Workhorse

Applied Epic is, without qualification, the deepest commercial AMS on the market for independent agencies in the 5-to-15 staff range. It is the platform most larger independent commercial agencies run on, and for good reason: it was architected for commercial workflow from the beginning, carries meaningful native automation capability, and offers the strongest API story of the four platforms evaluated here.

The platform's real strength, for our purposes, is its Activity Templates and Workflow Manager features. These allow an agency to define standard workflow sequences — a renewal stewardship cadence, a new-client onboarding sequence, a post-claim follow-up pattern — as reusable templates that attach to activities in the AMS. Most Applied Epic users never configure these deeply; they are the untapped native capability that already exists inside the platform and typically requires a skilled consultant or internal operator to unlock.

COI generation on Applied Epic is materially stronger than on EZLynx. The certificate module is tightly integrated with policy data, endorsement scheduling is supported at the form-number level, and the description-of-operations box can be templated by certificate holder. First-pass rejection rates for Applied Epic agencies with properly configured certificate modules typically run well below the EZLynx baseline — though the configuration work is non-trivial and is frequently not done.

Commercial new business submission is well-supported via Applied's integrated submission workflows and carrier-specific templates. Multi-market submission is workable, though most agencies augment with external submission management tools for efficiency.

API extensibility is the strongest of the four. Applied Epic offers integration endpoints that are deep and stable enough to support serious workflow automation built in external tools. Most significant commercial agency automation work in the market today happens on Applied Epic books via API integration.

Commission reconciliation is adequate natively; the accounting module is deep enough that larger agencies run their general ledger directly in Applied Epic rather than exporting to external accounting software. Commercial direct-bill reconciliation is still a manual-intensive workflow, but less so than on EZLynx.

The trade-offs with Applied Epic are cost and complexity. The platform is meaningfully more expensive than EZLynx or HawkSoft, both in licensing and in implementation cost. Implementation windows of three to six months are typical. The platform rewards agencies that invest in configuration and penalizes those that do not — an agency running Applied Epic with default configurations is often worse off than the same agency running EZLynx with tight configurations.

APPLIED EPIC · VERDICT

The commercial-focused AMS of choice for agencies willing to invest in configuration. Best ceiling, highest floor

Applied Epic is the strongest commercial-lines AMS for agencies in the $750K-plus commercial commission range, particularly for construction, trucking, and complex commercial books. The platform's real value is unlocked by proper configuration of Activity Templates, Workflow Manager, and the certificate module — work most agencies do not do, which is where external consulting and workflow layer investments pay outsized returns.

Best for: Commercial-focused agencies with $750K+ commercial commission. Construction, trucking, and specialty-heavy books. Agencies willing to invest in deep configuration or bring in implementation help.

HawkSoft: The Integration-First Platform

HawkSoft sits in an interesting middle position. The platform is less expensive than Applied Epic, more commercial-capable than EZLynx for mid-sized books, and — critically — built around a philosophy of partner integrations rather than monolithic feature ownership. Where Applied Epic tries to do everything in-platform and EZLynx ships a constrained set of native tools, HawkSoft explicitly leans into a marketplace of partner integrations covering marketing automation, client portals, commission reconciliation, and workflow tooling.

This design philosophy is either a strength or a weakness depending on how the agency operates. For agencies that want to assemble their workflow stack from best-in-class components — Applied Marketing Automation for marketing, a purpose-built commission tool for reconciliation, a specific client portal — HawkSoft is the most cooperative AMS of the four. For agencies that want a single system to handle everything natively, HawkSoft feels undersupported.

COI generation on HawkSoft is capable with proper configuration but requires more manual oversight than Applied Epic. The certificate module is functional; the automation of certificate-holder-specific requirements is limited natively and typically gets augmented via partner integrations or external workflow tools.

Commercial new business submission is adequately supported, with strong carrier integration via the partner ecosystem. Multi-market submission is workable.

API extensibility is solid — not as deep as Applied Epic, but meaningfully better than EZLynx for workflow automation purposes. The partner ecosystem actively supports integration patterns, which means external workflow tools tend to have documented integration paths.

Commission reconciliation follows the pattern: native capability is modest; the partner ecosystem fills the gap.

The Agency Intelligence reporting module is a genuine differentiator — it provides the cleanest out-of-box view into commercial book health of any of the four platforms, with particular strength in retention analytics.

HAWKSOFT · VERDICT

The cooperative platform for agencies comfortable assembling a workflow stack from components

HawkSoft is a strong choice for commercial-focused agencies in the $500K to $1M commercial commission range that are comfortable with a component-based approach to workflow. The platform is cooperative with external tools, the Agency Intelligence reporting is genuinely useful, and the partner ecosystem is active. The trade-off is that assembling the stack requires more decision-making than adopting Applied Epic's native tooling.

Best for: Mid-sized commercial agencies that value component-based flexibility. Agencies that already run external tools and want their AMS to cooperate rather than compete.

AMS360: The Accounting-First Platform

AMS360 (part of Vertafore) is the platform that agencies migrate to when accounting and financial management are the primary constraint, not workflow or automation. The platform's accounting depth is real — AMS360 agencies routinely run their full general ledger in the platform, handle complex agency-bill billing, and manage trust accounting without external tools. For agencies where financial complexity is the primary driver — high-volume agency-bill books, complex commission structures, trust account requirements — AMS360 is a serious contender.

For commercial workflow automation specifically, however, AMS360 is the weakest of the four. The platform's automation orientation is accounting-first, not workflow-first, and several specific gaps show up for commercial-focused agencies.

COI generation on AMS360 is functional but frequently requires external augmentation for high-volume construction books. The certificate module is less integrated with policy data than Applied Epic's or HawkSoft's, and the automation of certificate-specific requirements is limited.

Commercial new business submission is supported via Vertafore's broader product ecosystem (TransactNOW, PL Rating for personal, various commercial tooling), but the integration across these components is not as tight as a single-platform experience.

API extensibility exists but has historically been less developer-friendly than Applied Epic or HawkSoft. The Vertafore ecosystem is moving toward more open integration, but the gap remains.

Commission reconciliation is strong — this is where AMS360 shines. The accounting depth makes commission reconciliation more tractable than on any of the other three platforms, which is why many accounting-forward agencies run AMS360 even when the workflow tooling is not ideal.

WorkSmart document management, the AMS360 document module, is capable but operates somewhat independently of the rest of the platform — agencies that rely on WorkSmart heavily sometimes find themselves running two parallel systems (policy data in AMS360, documents in WorkSmart) with friction at the boundary.

AMS360 · VERDICT

The accounting-first platform. Strong for financial complexity, weakest for commercial workflow automation

AMS360 is a pragmatic choice for agencies where financial complexity is the dominant consideration — heavy agency-bill books, trust accounting, complex commission structures. For commercial workflow automation specifically, it is the weakest of the four major platforms, and agencies running commercial books on AMS360 typically invest heavily in external workflow layers.

Best for: Agencies with high agency-bill complexity or trust accounting requirements. Agencies where the CFO or accounting function is a primary decision-maker. Larger agencies where accounting depth outweighs workflow depth.

The Decision Tree

Stripping the comparison down to the actual decision most commercial agency principals are making, the logic is:

If you are under $750K in commercial commission and mixed personal/commercial: EZLynx is likely fine. Invest the money you save in a lean workflow layer for COI generation and commission reconciliation. The ROI on a workflow automation layer on top of EZLynx is typically strong because the baseline capability is thin enough that any improvement is visible.

If you are above $750K in commercial commission, construction or trucking-heavy: Applied Epic is the right platform, provided you are willing to invest in configuration. Do not adopt Applied Epic and run it with defaults — you will pay premium licensing for a platform you are not using to its potential. The implementation work and the workflow configuration work pay back substantially over a five-year horizon.

If you want a component-based approach and value ecosystem cooperation: HawkSoft. Particularly strong for agencies that already run a set of external tools and want their AMS to interoperate rather than compete. The Agency Intelligence reporting alone is a genuine differentiator.

If accounting complexity is your dominant constraint: AMS360. Be prepared to invest in external workflow automation; the platform will not deliver workflow depth on its own for commercial operations.

"The platform you choose determines what you have to build around it. No platform eliminates the workflow layer — they just define its shape."

The Emerging Platforms — A Brief Note

Three newer platforms deserve mention, though none have yet reached the market share of the four discussed above for independent commercial agencies in the 5–15 staff range.

Momentum (formerly NowCerts) is a modern, API-first AMS that has earned a real following among commercial agencies seeking native automation. The platform's architecture is more contemporary than the others — it was built from the ground up for API integration, it offers native automation capabilities (particularly around SMS and email automation via Sonant integration), and its pricing is attractive. The platform is growing quickly in the independent commercial space. The trade-off is platform maturity and the ecosystem around it: Applied Epic has two decades of implementation consultants, integration partners, and staffed-up expertise in the agency market. Momentum is earning that ecosystem but is not there yet.

QQCatalyst, built on Salesforce, offers interesting flexibility for agencies that value the Salesforce platform's extensibility. For commercial workflow specifically, it is lighter than Applied Epic and HawkSoft.

Vertafore's newer offerings (including Vertafore Agency Platform) are evolving the AMS360 ecosystem but have not materially changed the commercial workflow evaluation for most independent agencies.

For an agency making a platform decision in 2026, the right frame is: the four platforms in this comparison cover roughly 85% of the independent commercial agency market. Momentum is worth evaluating if modern architecture and native automation are high-priority. The other newer platforms are not yet the mainstream choice for commercial-focused independent agencies.

What to Actually Do Next

Most agencies reading this article are not actually in the market to switch platforms. The more common situation is an agency running one of these four platforms, wondering whether the gaps they experience daily are structural (meaning the platform cannot close them) or configuration-level (meaning the platform can close them but is not currently). The distinction matters because the first situation implies either switching platforms or building an external workflow layer, and the second implies hiring a consultant to unlock capability that already exists.

A practical diagnostic: for each of the five commercial workflows listed at the beginning of this article — COI generation, new business submission, renewal stewardship, commission reconciliation, cross-sell triggers — ask whether the current experience on your platform is (a) working natively, (b) working with a workaround, (c) not working at all. The distribution of answers across those five workflows tells you where you are.

An agency on Applied Epic with all five workflows in category (b) is typically a configuration problem, not a platform problem. An agency on EZLynx with three or more in category (c) is typically at the edge of the platform's capability and either needs an automation layer above it or should consider migration. An agency on HawkSoft with mixed (a) and (b) is usually operating the platform well and will benefit most from strengthening specific partner integrations.

Platform migration is a rare event and an expensive one; the more common and typically higher-ROI move is the automation layer above the AMS you already run.
